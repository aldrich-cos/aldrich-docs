The one-line positioning

A personal AI chief of staff.

Aldrich remembers what matters, surfaces what you'd miss, and acts on your behalf across your apps — so your mind is free for the work only you can do.

  • "Personal AI chief of staff" — category anchor; familiar role, intuitive trust posture.
  • "Remembers what matters" — the memory pillar; differentiator from stateless ChatGPT.
  • "Surfaces what you'd miss" — proactive behavior; differentiator from reactive assistants.
  • "Acts on your behalf" — agentic capability; differentiator from notes and chat.
  • "Free for work only you can do" — the felt benefit.

Persona 1 — What Maya wants Aldrich to do

Five jobs Maya is hiring Aldrich for.

  • Be my memory. Know what I told Alex last Tuesday so I don't look like a jerk.
  • Prep me. Before my 9am, tell me the three things that matter and the context I need.
  • Execute small tasks. Schedule, reschedule, reply-ack, draft, add a task — without me opening every app.
  • Surface the needle. Tell me the 3 things I'd have missed today; hide the noise.
  • Give me a quiet mind. Let me trust nothing's slipping so I can do deep work without guilt.
She becomes a promoter when

"I genuinely rely on it. I stopped missing commitments. I got my evenings back. It prepped me for a board meeting better than my Chief of Staff would have."

Persona 2

Sam, the independent operator.

Solo consultant or agency owner, age 35–55. 10+ clients, every hour tracked, every admin minute costs money.

  • Remember every client's context — who they are, what was promised last.

Jordan, the creator-operator.

Content creator or solo SaaS founder, age 25–40. Lives in the browser and phone. Values speed, autonomy, self-direction. Comp variable but often high and lumpy.

Audience boundaries

Who Aldrich is NOT for in V1.

  • Enterprise teams — deferred to V3 (needs SSO, admin console, DPAs).
  • Developers seeking an API — V2 roadmap.
  • People wanting a ChatGPT replacement for general Q&A — Aldrich is opinionated about proactivity and memory; ChatGPT is better if you don't want agency.
  • Privacy purists wanting zero cloud — Aldrich is cloud-heavy with selective on-device; Kagi Assistant or local-only tools fit better.
  • Users wanting a life coach or therapist — Aldrich is not that and shouldn't pretend.

Secondary, but welcomed: students & early-career on Free; retirees managing complex lives (later); neurodivergent users needing executive-function support — they will find us, but we won't design specifically for them in V1.

Three-benefit stack

Memory. Awareness. Action.

1. Remembers

Your conversations, commitments, people, preferences — Aldrich keeps track, across apps, across time.

2. Stays ahead

Morning briefings, meeting prep, well-timed nudges. Everything you'd have missed, surfaced before you need it.

3. Acts, carefully

When you give it the reins, it schedules, drafts, replies, organizes. When unsure, it asks. You're always in control.

Brand voice

Capable, discreet, warm, honest about limits.

Voice pillars

  • Capable, not show-offy. Direct, understated, confident.
  • Discreet. Knows everything, says nothing it shouldn't.
  • Human-warm, not chatty. Relational, never gushy.
  • Honest about limits. "I'm not sure — want me to draft it?" beats "Sure! Done!"

Don'ts

  • No "game-changing," "revolutionary," "mind-blowing."
  • No emoji overload.
  • No "powered by AI" as a value prop.
  • No "imagine if..." copy. Show, don't imagine.
  • No public dunks on competitors by name.

Aldrich · Target User & Positioning
Category-of-one

Every other product is a tool. Aldrich is a role.

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini are general intelligence rentals — you ask, they answer, they forget. Productivity apps store information but you go to them. Voice assistants do commands without memory or context.

A chief of staff is categorically different: memory across interactions, proactive intelligence, limited agency to act, trusted discretion. Until now that role has been purely human, available only to a handful. Aldrich is what happens when the role becomes software.

Why this framing wins

Resonates with the buyer (execs already wish they had one). Sets appropriate expectations. Justifies the price ($120K human vs. $18/mo). Creates a moat in words — first to plant the flag owns the associative space.

Anti-positioning

Things we'll be tempted to say — and shouldn't.

  • "We're the ChatGPT for X." No — we're not a ChatGPT variant.
  • "We're an autonomous AI agent." No — autonomous is scary. "Acts carefully on your behalf" is the frame.
  • "We're the all-in-one platform for..." No — we're opinionated and narrow.
  • "We replace your VA / Chief of Staff." No — we augment, and make the role accessible to those who don't have one.
  • "We learn from all your data." Too creepy. "We remember what you tell us and what you let us see."

Trust as positioning

Posture, not just features.

  • Your data stays yours. No training without explicit opt-in. Clear export. Clear delete. Verifiable.
  • It acts only when you approve. Default is ask-first for anything consequential. You control the autonomy dial.
  • You can always see and always undo. Activity log, memory viewer, 30-day undo stack.
  • We tell you when we're uncertain. Confidence is built into copy.

Every competitor could ship a "delete" button. None can credibly say "your data stays yours" the way a privacy-native startup can.

The success test

We'll know positioning is working when…

  • Users describe us in our own words — 3 of 5 interviewed users say "it's like a chief of staff" without prompting.
  • Wrong-fit users self-filter — free-trial-to-paid stays healthy; we don't churn out people we mis-sold.
  • We stop being compared to ChatGPT as category awareness grows.
  • Press uses our framing — not "another AI assistant" but "the AI chief of staff" in headlines.
  • Internal discipline holds — every landing page, tweet, and deck uses the same three-benefit stack.
The ideal-user gut check

When tempted to add a feature, change positioning, or chase a segment: does Maya want this? If yes, probably do it. If no, probably don't. If she'd actively dislike it, definitely don't.
